# 💎 Permanent Notes

Permanent notes are the heart of your second brain—refined, atomic ideas written in your own words that form your personal knowledge base.

## Purpose

- **Capture understanding**: Ideas you've internalized
- **Build connections**: Link ideas to create knowledge networks
- **Enable emergence**: Let insights arise from connections
- **Preserve thinking**: Your intellectual work, distilled

## What Makes a Note "Permanent"

### The Five Characteristics

1. **Atomic**: Contains one clear, focused idea
2. **Autonomous**: Can stand alone without context
3. **Connected**: Links to 1-2 related notes minimum
4. **Your words**: Written in your own understanding
5. **Timeless**: Not bound to specific projects or time periods

### Permanent vs. Other Note Types

| Aspect | Permanent | Fleeting | Literature | Project |
|--------|-----------|----------|------------|---------|
| **Perspective** | Your words | Quick capture | Source's words | Temporal work |
| **Lifespan** | Indefinite | Days/weeks | Reference | Months |
| **Scope** | One idea | Rough thoughts | Source summary | Goal-oriented |
| **Links** | Essential | Optional | Forward links | Contextual |
| **Refinement** | High | Low | Medium | Medium-High |

## When to Create Permanent Notes

✅ **Create permanent notes when:**
- You've understood an idea deeply enough to explain it
- An idea connects to your existing knowledge
- You want to remember this insight long-term
- The idea could be useful in multiple contexts
- You've transformed a source idea into your own understanding

❌ **Don't create permanent notes for:**
- Ideas you haven't fully understood yet (keep in inbox)
- Direct source material (use literature notes)
- Project-specific information (use project notes)
- Temporary reminders or tasks

## Permanent Note Workflow

### 1. Source → Understanding

**Transform these into permanent notes:**
- Insights from literature notes
- Revelations from daily reflections
- Connections you discover between ideas
- Lessons from project experiences
- Original thoughts and observations

### 2. Write Atomically

**One permanent note = One idea**

✅ Good:
```
# 20260421150000-compound-effect-of-learning

Small, consistent learning compounds over time like financial
interest. Daily reading of 30 minutes accumulates to 182 hours
per year—roughly 36 books.

The key is consistency, not intensity.
```

❌ Too broad:
```
# Learning Strategies

Here are 15 different ways to learn better...
[Multiple unrelated ideas]
```

### 3. Write Clearly

**Write for your future self:**
- Use clear, simple language
- Explain context briefly
- Include examples
- Define terms if specialized
- Write complete thoughts, not fragments

### 4. Create Connections

**Every permanent note should link to 1-2 others minimum**

**Types of connections:**
- **Supports**: Reinforces another idea
- **Contradicts**: Opposes another idea
- **Exemplifies**: Specific instance of a concept
- **Extends**: Builds upon another idea
- **Questions**: Challenges another idea

### 5. Add to MOCs

**When creating/updating permanent notes:**
1. Check relevant MOCs
2. Add note to appropriate section
3. Consider if note reveals need for new MOC (10+ related notes)

## Naming Convention

**Format**: `YYYYMMDDHHMMSS-descriptive-title.md`

**Why timestamp prefix?**
- Ensures uniqueness (no filename conflicts)
- Chronological sorting possible
- Separates creation time from content evolution

**Examples:**
- ✅ `20260421143022-spaced-repetition-learning.md`
- ✅ `20260421150000-attention-as-finite-resource.md`
- ✅ `20260421153000-compounding-knowledge.md`

**Title guidelines:**
- 3-7 words
- Descriptive and specific
- Use lowercase with hyphens
- Focus on the concept, not the source

## Permanent Note Structure

### Minimal Structure
Every permanent note should have:
1. **Title**: Clear, specific concept name
2. **Main idea**: The core concept explained
3. **Context**: Where this idea came from or why it matters
4. **Connections**: Links to related notes

### Recommended Template Sections
- **Context**: Origin of the idea
- **Main Idea**: The atomic concept
- **Supporting Details**: Evidence, examples, elaboration
- **Connections**: Related notes (1-2 minimum)
- **Questions**: What remains to explore
- **Sources**: Literature notes or references

## Quality Checklist

### Before marking a note as "evergreen":

- [ ] **Atomic**: Contains exactly one idea?
- [ ] **Clear title**: Specific and descriptive?
- [ ] **Your words**: Written in your own understanding?
- [ ] **Stands alone**: Understandable without other notes?
- [ ] **Connected**: Links to 1-2+ related notes?
- [ ] **Contextualized**: Explains where the idea came from?
- [ ] **Valuable**: Worth remembering long-term?

## Organization

### Flat Structure (Recommended)

**Keep `03-Permanent/` flat:**
- Don't create subfolders
- Use links and MOCs for organization
- Let connections define structure, not folders

**Why flat?**
- Ideas don't fit into neat categories
- Forcing hierarchy limits connection-making
- Graph-based organization is more flexible
- MOCs provide needed navigation

### When to Add Subfolders

**Only if you have 1000+ permanent notes**, consider:
```
03-Permanent/
├── 2026/
├── 2027/
└── 2028/
```

Year-based folders keep file listings manageable while maintaining flat structure within years.

## Common Pitfalls

### Anti-Patterns to Avoid

❌ **Copying instead of processing**
- Literature notes → copy/paste → call it permanent
- ✅ Instead: Read, understand, write in your own words

❌ **No connections**
- Creating isolated permanent notes
- ✅ Instead: Always link to 1-2 related notes

❌ **Too broad**
- Trying to cover multiple ideas in one note
- ✅ Instead: Split into multiple atomic notes

❌ **Too specific**
- Notes tied to specific projects or contexts
- ✅ Instead: Extract timeless principles

❌ **Perfectionism**
- Waiting for the "perfect" note before creating it
- ✅ Instead: Write draft, tag #status/draft, refine over time

❌ **Premature permanence**
- Moving from inbox to permanent too quickly
- ✅ Instead: Let ideas marinate, ensure understanding

## Progressive Elaboration

**Permanent notes can evolve:**

**v1**: Basic capture (#status/draft)
```markdown
# Idea

Brief explanation. Rough notes.

Related: [[other-note]]
```

**v2**: Elaborated (#status/evergreen)
```markdown
# Clear Title

Explanation with context. Supporting details.
Examples included.

Connected to: [[note-1]], [[note-2]]
Questions to explore: ...
```

**v3**: Refined and linked
```markdown
Well-structured note with all sections.
Multiple connections established.
Referenced in MOCs.
Linked from other notes.
```

## Linking Best Practices

### Minimum Connections
- **Every permanent note**: 1-2 links minimum
- **Well-integrated note**: 3-5 links
- **Hub note**: 6+ links (consider making it a MOC)

### Types of Links

**Internal links** (to your notes):
```markdown
Related to: [[other-permanent-note]]
Example of: [[broader-concept]]
Contradicts: [[opposing-view]]
Extends: [[foundational-idea]]
```

**Source links** (to literature):
```markdown
Source: [[Author-Year-Book]]
Referenced in: [[literature-note]]
```

**MOC links**:
```markdown
Part of: [[Topic-MOC]]
See also: [[Related-Topic-MOC]]
```

### Linking Strategy

1. **As you write**: Add obvious links immediately
2. **During weekly review**: Add connections you discover
3. **During monthly review**: Check for orphaned notes
4. **Use backlinks**: See what links TO this note

## Finding Notes

### Obsidian Tools

**Search**: `Cmd/Ctrl + Shift + F`
- Search all note content
- Use regex for advanced searches

**Quick Switcher**: `Cmd/Ctrl + O`
- Jump to note by name
- Fuzzy matching

**Graph View**
- Visual network of connections
- Identify clusters and gaps

**Backlinks Panel**
- See what links to current note
- Discover unlinked mentions

### Tags for Discovery

**Status tags:**
- `#status/draft` - Work in progress
- `#status/evergreen` - Refined and stable

**Use sparingly**—prefer links over tags

## Growth Patterns

### Healthy Growth

✅ **Regular creation**: 2-3 permanent notes per week
✅ **Increasing connections**: New notes link to existing
✅ **Emerging MOCs**: Clusters of related notes form naturally
✅ **Revisiting notes**: Adding links to older notes
✅ **Quality over quantity**: Refined understanding, not just volume

### Warning Signs

⚠️ **No new permanent notes for 2+ weeks**: Not capturing learning
⚠️ **Many orphaned notes**: Not creating connections
⚠️ **All notes tagged #draft**: Not refining ideas
⚠️ **No MOCs emerging**: Not finding patterns
⚠️ **Duplicates**: Creating similar notes without linking

## Review Process

### Weekly Review (Permanent Notes)
- Create 2-3 new permanent notes from inbox/fleeting
- Add connections to new notes
- Refine 1-2 draft notes to evergreen

### Monthly Review (Permanent Notes)
- Check for orphaned notes (no incoming links)
- Identify emerging topics (10+ related notes → new MOC)
- Refine older draft notes
- Update Master Index with note count

# Spaced Repetition Improves Retention

## Context

Discovered while reading [[Oakley-2014-Learning-How-To-Learn]]. The
forgetting curve shows we forget ~70% of new information within 24
hours unless reviewed.

## Main Idea

Reviewing information at increasing intervals (e.g., 1 day, 3 days,
1 week, 2 weeks) significantly improves long-term retention compared
to massed practice (cramming).

The key is timing reviews just as you're about to forget—this
strengthens the memory trace.

## Supporting Details

- Optimal intervals: 1 day → 3 days → 1 week → 2 weeks → 1 month
- Works by forcing active recall during review
- More effective than passive re-reading
- Particularly powerful for factual knowledge
